Fermat's Little Theorem

Fermat's Little Theorem states that for any prime \(p\) and integer \(a\) such that \(p \nmid a\):

\(a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod p.\)

Euler's Totient Function

Euler's Theorem is a generalization of Fermat's Little Theorem that states for coprime \(a\) and \(n\):

\(a^{\phi (n)} \equiv 1 \pmod{n}\)

in which \(\phi (n)\) is Euler's Totient Function, which returns the number of integers less than or equal to \(n\) that are also coprime to \(n\). To find \(\phi (n)\), find the prime factors of \(n\) and multiply \(n\) by \(1\) less than each of them and then divide n by all of them.

Application

The most obvious application of Euler's Totient Function is to evaluate the remainders of numbers with large exponents. For example, we can easily find the last two digits of \(17^{561}\) by noting that \(17^{40} \equiv 1 \pmod {100}\) because \(\phi(100)=40\). Since \(561\equiv 1 \pmod{40},\) the last two digits of \(17^{561}\) are \(17\).
